The Soul of GroomOS

This is the design philosophy Paul wrote for the project. It's the "why" behind every decision. Think of it as the rulebook for what GroomOS should feel like.

The Guiding Question
"How do we build software that serves the groomer — not the other way around?"

Every design choice, every screen, every button traces back to this. The groomer's day is physical and exhausting. The software should feel like relief — like stepping into a cool room after being outside all day.

🎵 The Rick Rubin Principle

Rick Rubin is a famous music producer. His philosophy is: don't mess with the artist's raw material. Just help it shine.

In GroomOS, the "artist" is the groomer. When a groomer finishes a session, they might type something like:

"Great session, I love him! FCG + Belly + paws + compacted coat brush out. $220. Mum is getting MARRIED this weekend! Recommended to see again in 4 months, around mid July."

That one paragraph contains five different types of information — pet feelings, services done, pricing, client personal news, and scheduling advice. A traditional app would force the groomer to type that into five separate boxes.

GroomOS doesn't do that. The groomer types naturally. The AI reads it behind the scenes and quietly files everything into the right categories. The groomer's words stay exactly as written — forever. The software does the organising.

📖 The Narrative Principle

Individual facts are useful. But the story is what matters.

A cat that's been groomed 12 times builds a history. Maybe the first few visits they were nervous and needed breaks. By visit 6, much calmer. By visit 10, a total angel who purrs through the whole session. That's a narrative arc — and it tells the groomer everything they need to know.

A great groomer carries all this in their head — but with 200+ clients, memory fails. The AI doesn't forget. It's a relationship memory, not a chatbot.

🎨 How GroomOS Should Look

These are the visual rules. Every screen, every component follows these.

🔵
All corners are rounded. Always. Apple design philosophy — soft, inviting, never sharp. This is why everything in GroomOS has rounded edges.
🪟
Glassmorphism. Frosted glass effects — things look like they're made of semi-transparent glass floating above the page. Blur, depth, translucent layers. The signature GroomOS aesthetic.
☀️
One sun per page. Every element is lit by the same imaginary light source (top-left by default). Highlights, shadows, glass reflections — all consistent. If one card is lit from the left and another from the right, it looks wrong even if you can't say why.
🫧
Pop-ups float. Modals, slide-ins, drawers — they should look like they're hovering above the surface. A gentle shadow, breathing room. Never cramped.
🚪
Every overlay has a way back. No dead ends. Every popup, drawer, or panel has a close button, a cancel option, or a way to go back. The user should never feel trapped.
🧅
Progressive disclosure. Show what matters first. Details on demand. Don't overwhelm people with everything at once — like peeling layers of an onion. The most important info is visible immediately; dig deeper if you need to.
📎
The AI is like a discrete Clippy. Remember Clippy from old Microsoft Word? Like that, but actually good. Floating on screen, present but not obnoxious. It draws attention through subtle movement, not shouting.

🎨 DRIP — Custom Themes

DRIP is the theming system. It lets users customise the look of their GroomOS — colours, patterns, personality. Think of it like changing the wallpaper on your phone, but way more detailed.

What's built so far: colour palette editor for booking tickets, day column colours, day-off pattern selector, global accent colour, and three preset themes (Classic, Midnight, Cherry Blossom).

The big dream — "Wraps": full visual themes that completely transform the interface. Paul has two wild ideas: a mid-century modern wrap with retro typography, and a "Cat is the customer" wrap where everything on screen plays into the fantasy that YOU are a cat booking a groom. Buttons might wiggle (cats love movement), design circles could become cat food bowls, and sometimes another cat walks across the background.

🐱 The AI Characters

Miso — the internal brain

Miso is the mascot cat you've already met (on your handbook icon!). In the software, Miso is the AI engine that reads grooming notes, builds pet histories, predicts how long sessions will take, and generally makes the groomer's life easier. Miso works behind the scenes — quiet, smart, always learning.

Via — the digital receptionist

Via is the client-facing AI that handles booking requests. She chats with pet owners, follows Paul's booking rules, and handles 80%+ of scheduling automatically. Helpful, clear, professional but warm. She and Miso share the same brain but have different audiences.

💜 The Brand Feeling

"You manage pets' energy all day.
Let us help you with yours.

Intuitive landscape. Everything just... works.
It's the crème de la crème of UI — and it's beautiful."

That's Paul's brand-voice seed. The tension between the groomer's physical, exhausting work and the calm, effortless digital experience. The glass aesthetic, the soft depth, the quiet intelligence — it all serves this feeling of relief.

Glassmorphism

GroomOS's signature look. Think frosted glass — like looking through a bathroom window. Elements feel like they're floating on translucent layers.

Play with it — drag the slider to change the blur

I'm a glass card
Blur: 16px
What makes it "glass"?

Three ingredients: a semi-transparent background (you can see through it), a blur effect (so what's behind looks frosted), and a subtle border that catches the "light."

When do we use it?

Panels, cards, navigation bars, modals — basically any surface that "floats" above the page. It creates a sense of depth without being heavy.

The "One Sun" Rule ☀️

This is one of Paul's golden rules. Every element on a page is lit by the same imaginary sun — usually up and to the left. No element gets its own special lighting.

Right now: All three cards have their highlight (bright edge) on the top-left and their shadow falling bottom-right. They look like they belong on the same page. Click the cards to see what happens when each card has its own light source.

The simple version: Imagine the sun is shining through a window at the top-left of the screen. Every card, button, and panel should have its bright edge toward that window, and its shadow going the other way. If one element breaks this, it looks "off" even if you can't explain why.

How We Name Things

Every button in the app gets a name made of three parts. This means when Paul says "use the primary-solid-lilac button here," everyone knows exactly which one he means — no screenshots needed.

The recipe is: [role]-[treatment]-[accent]. Pick one from each group below and watch the preview update. Click again to unselect.

Step 1: Role — what's the button's job?

Think of a webpage like a team. Each button has a different job to do.

The main action. The thing the page most wants you to do. Like "Save," "Book Appointment," or "Confirm." Usually the biggest, boldest button on the page. Every page typically has just one.
The backup option. Things like "Cancel," "Go Back," or "Skip." They're there if you need them, but they don't shout for attention. Quieter, less bold than primary.
Warning — this does something permanent. "Delete," "Remove," "Deactivate." Usually red, because red = caution. You see these on confirmation popups when something can't be undone.
A small helper. Close buttons (the ✕), settings cogs, little "+ Add" links. Not a main action — just a tool that sits quietly until you need it.

Step 2: Treatment — what does it look like?

Same job, different outfit. A "Save" button could be a solid block of colour or a subtle outline — the treatment is which style it wears.

Filled in with colour. A solid block — the most attention-grabbing style. Like a painted sign that says "PRESS ME." You can't miss it.
Frosted glass effect. Semi-see-through with a blur — the GroomOS signature look. Fancy and modern, but still readable. Like looking through a bathroom window.
Just a border, no fill. The inside is transparent — you only see the edge and the text. Quieter than solid. Good for secondary actions that shouldn't compete with the main button.
Almost invisible — just text. No border, no background. Looks like a text link. The quietest option. Used for things like "Skip for now" where you barely want people to notice it.

Step 3: Accent — what colour?

The colour gives it personality and helps group things visually.

GroomOS signature purple. The brand colour. When in doubt, lilac is almost always the right choice for primary actions.
Red/orange — for warnings. Paired with danger buttons. Says "be careful" without screaming. Delete, remove, cancel subscription — that kind of thing.
Neutral grey. Blends in quietly. Used for secondary and utility buttons that shouldn't distract from the main action.
Near-black. Bold and serious. Currently used on some primary buttons in GroomOS — strong contrast on light backgrounds.
